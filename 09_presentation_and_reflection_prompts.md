# Presentation and Reflection Prompts

## Purpose

This document provides prompts for the final presentation and reflection session used in the educational experience.

The purpose of this session is to support classroom discussion of the artifacts produced by each team, with special attention to the connection between product vision, product backlog, UML models, sustainability-oriented requirements, acceptance criteria, and verification evidence.

The session is also intended to help students reflect on what they learned, what difficulties they encountered, and how their understanding of the project changed after revisiting it through a sustainability-oriented lens.

## Session format

The presentation and reflection session may be conducted after teams complete the sustainability-oriented requirements activity.

In the reported educational experience, this moment involved an in-person session for presentation and discussion of results.

Each team should briefly present its project and explain how sustainability concerns were identified and transformed into requirements-related elements.

## Suggested presentation structure

Each team may organize its presentation according to the following structure:

1. Brief overview of the project
2. Main users and stakeholders
3. Main elements of the product vision
4. Relevant backlog items or user stories
5. UML diagrams used to support reasoning
6. Sustainability concerns identified
7. Sustainability dimensions addressed
8. Non-functional requirements derived from these concerns
9. Acceptance criteria
10. Possible verification evidence
11. Difficulties encountered
12. Revisions or improvements that would be made after the activity

## Presentation prompts

Teams may use the following prompts to prepare their presentation.

### Project overview

- What problem does the project address?
- Who are the main users and stakeholders?
- What is the expected value of the solution?
- What is the intended context of use?

### Connection with previous artifacts

- Which elements of the product vision helped identify sustainability concerns?
- Which backlog items revealed implicit quality or sustainability concerns?
- Which UML diagrams supported the analysis?
- Did the team identify inconsistencies or missing requirements when revisiting previous artifacts?

### Sustainability concerns

- Which sustainability concerns were identified?
- Which sustainability dimensions were considered?
- Why were these concerns relevant to the project context?
- Were any concerns initially implicit or overlooked?

### Non-functional requirements

- How did the team transform sustainability concerns into non-functional requirements?
- How are the requirements connected to the product vision, backlog, or UML models?
- Are the requirements specific to the project context?
- Are they actionable and reviewable?

### Acceptance criteria and verification evidence

- What acceptance criteria were proposed?
- What evidence could be used to verify or assess the requirements?
- Could the evidence involve tests, inspections, checklists, logs, metrics, usability evaluation, accessibility review, or stakeholder feedback?
- Which part was most difficult: identifying the concern, writing the requirement, defining the acceptance criterion, or proposing verification evidence?

### Difficulties and lessons learned

- What was the easiest part of the activity?
- What was the most difficult part of the activity?
- Which sustainability dimension was easiest to address?
- Which sustainability dimension was most difficult to address?
- Did the activity change the team's understanding of the project?
- What would the team revise in the product vision, backlog, or UML models after this activity?

## Reflection prompts for students

Students may use the following prompts for individual or group reflection.

## Understanding of the project

- What did the team understand differently about the project after analyzing sustainability concerns?
- Did the activity reveal any missing user need, stakeholder concern, or quality attribute?
- Did the activity reveal any inconsistency between the product vision, backlog, and UML models?

## Sustainability-oriented reasoning

- Which sustainability dimension seemed most directly related to the project?
- Which sustainability dimension was least obvious at first?
- Did the team focus mainly on technical sustainability?
- How did the team consider individual or social sustainability?
- Were environmental or economic concerns difficult to identify? Why?

## Requirements learning

- Did the activity help distinguish functional and non-functional requirements?
- Did the team initially formulate broad or vague requirements?
- How were these requirements refined?
- Did the acceptance criteria help make the requirements more concrete?
- Did the verification evidence help connect requirements to testing, inspection, metrics, or review?

## Design and modeling

- Did the UML diagrams help identify sustainability concerns?
- Did the activity suggest any change in the system workflow, components, states, or responsibilities?
- Did the team identify any unnecessary steps, duplicated work, excessive dependencies, or unclear states?
- Did the activity reveal design decisions that could affect users or stakeholders over time?

## Future improvement

- What would the team do differently if the project continued?
- Which artifact should be revised first?
- What additional information would be needed from users or stakeholders?
- What type of evidence would be most useful to assess the proposed non-functional requirements?
- What should be considered in future development iterations?

## Instructor feedback prompts

The instructor may use the following prompts to guide discussion and feedback.

### About the project framing

- Is the problem clearly connected to a real context?
- Are users and stakeholders sufficiently identified?
- Is the expected value of the system clear?
- Are assumptions, constraints, and risks relevant to the sustainability discussion?

### About the backlog and models

- Are backlog items coherent with the product vision?
- Do user stories express user needs rather than technical tasks?
- Do UML diagrams help explain relevant aspects of the system?
- Do the models reveal quality concerns, missing requirements, or design issues?

### About sustainability-oriented requirements

- Is the sustainability concern specific to the project context?
- Is the selected sustainability dimension appropriate?
- Is the requirement formulated as a quality, constraint, or expected property?
- Is the requirement non-functional rather than merely functional?
- Is the requirement specific enough to be discussed and reviewed?
- Is the acceptance criterion assessable?
- Is the proposed verification evidence plausible?

### About learning and reflection

- What did the team learn from revisiting previous artifacts?
- What difficulty seems most relevant for future teaching iterations?
- Did the team demonstrate awareness of technical, human, organizational, and long-term impacts?
- What feedback could help the team refine its requirements further?

## Suggested discussion questions for the whole class

The following questions may be used after all team presentations.

- Which sustainability dimensions appeared most frequently across projects?
- Which dimensions were more difficult to identify?
- Were teams more comfortable with technical sustainability than with individual, social, economic, or environmental sustainability?
- What kinds of concerns emerged from product vision artifacts?
- What kinds of concerns emerged from backlog items?
- What kinds of concerns emerged from UML diagrams?
- What made some non-functional requirements more concrete than others?
- What types of verification evidence were easier to propose?
- What types of verification evidence were more difficult to propose?
- How can sustainability-oriented requirements improve software quality in digital health systems?
- How can these concerns be considered earlier in the development process?

## Expected output

After the presentation and reflection session, each team should be able to identify:

1. sustainability concerns relevant to its project;
2. at least one sustainability-oriented non-functional requirement;
3. at least one acceptance criterion;
4. at least one possible source of verification evidence;
5. at least one previous artifact that should be revised or improved;
6. at least one lesson learned from the activity.

## Connection to the educational experience

This presentation and reflection session supports the final stage of the educational experience.

It helps students consolidate the idea that Software Engineering artifacts are interconnected. Product vision, product backlog, UML models, non-functional requirements, acceptance criteria, and verification evidence are not isolated deliverables. They are complementary artifacts that support communication, analysis, feedback, refinement, and quality-oriented decision-making.

The session also reinforces the role of sustainability as a cross-cutting concern. By discussing different projects and dimensions, students can compare how technical, social, economic, environmental, and individual sustainability concerns emerge in digital health software projects.
