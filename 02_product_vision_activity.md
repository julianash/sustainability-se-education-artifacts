# Product Vision Activity

## Objective

The objective of this activity is to support students in framing the problem, identifying users and stakeholders, defining the expected value of the solution, and clarifying the scope, assumptions, constraints, risks, and boundaries of the project.

In the educational experience, the product vision was used as an initial artifact to help student teams move from a broad project idea to a more structured understanding of the problem, the intended software product, and its context of use.

## Learning goals

After completing this activity, students are expected to be able to:

- describe the problem or opportunity that motivates the project;
- explain why the problem is relevant;
- identify users, clients, partners, and other stakeholders;
- define the expected benefits and new capabilities to be delivered;
- describe the main features at a high level of abstraction;
- distinguish what is inside and outside the project scope;
- identify assumptions that affect planning and feasibility;
- identify constraints related to time, technology, costs, team, data, infrastructure, or deployment context;
- identify technical, organizational, and user-related risks;
- connect the product vision to later backlog, modeling, and sustainability-oriented requirements activities.

## Student task

Each team should prepare a product vision document addressing the following elements:

1. Project justification
2. Project objective
3. Project scope
4. Out-of-scope elements
5. Assumptions
6. Constraints
7. Risks
8. Main stakeholders

The product vision should be written at a level of abstraction that supports communication with stakeholders and guides later Software Engineering activities. It should not be a detailed technical specification.

## Suggested structure

| Section | Purpose | Guiding questions |
|---|---|---|
| Project justification | Explain the problem or opportunity that motivates the project. | What problem does the project address? Why is this problem relevant? Why is it worth developing this software? In digital health projects, is the context of use clearly explained? |
| Project objective | Describe the benefits and new capabilities expected from the software. | What does the project intend to deliver? What is the main expected benefit for users, clients, or stakeholders? Is the objective coherent with the justification? |
| Project scope | Describe, at a high level, the main features that will be part of the software. | What are the main features? Are they coherent with the objective? Is the scope feasible for the course context? |
| Out of scope | Clarify what will not be delivered, especially items that may generate undue expectations. | What will not be part of the system? Which items should be explicitly excluded to avoid ambiguity with stakeholders? |
| Assumptions | Identify factors considered true for planning or estimating the project. | What assumptions are being made about users, client participation, data, infrastructure, availability, or context of use? Are these assumptions realistic? |
| Constraints | Identify internal or external factors that limit project decisions. | What limitations affect the project? Are there constraints related to time, technology, costs, team, data, infrastructure, or deployment? |
| Risks | Identify potential problems and possible responses. | What technical, organizational, or user-related risks may affect the project? Is there a contingency or mitigation strategy for the most relevant risks? |
| Main stakeholders | Identify the people or groups involved in or affected by the project. | Who are the users, clients, partners, or other interested parties? Is anyone important missing? |

## Suggested template

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| Project justification | Describe the problem or opportunity that motivates the project and explain its relevance. |
| Project objective | Describe what the project intends to deliver and the expected benefits for users, clients, or stakeholders. |
| Project scope | Describe the main features that will be part of the software at a high level of abstraction. |
| Out-of-scope elements | Describe what will not be delivered, especially items that may generate undue expectations. |
| Assumptions | Describe factors considered true for planning or estimating the project. |
| Constraints | Describe internal or external factors that limit project decisions. |
| Risks | Describe potential problems and possible contingency or mitigation actions. |
| Main stakeholders | Identify the main users, clients, partners, or other interested parties and their roles. |

## Review and self-assessment

After preparing the product vision, each team should review the artifact using a self-assessment instrument.

The review should consider whether:

- the problem is clearly described;
- the relevance of the problem is explicit;
- the context of use is well explained, especially in digital health projects;
- the project objective describes what the software intends to deliver;
- the benefits for users, clients, or stakeholders are clear;
- the objective is coherent with the project justification;
- the main features are described at a high level of abstraction;
- the scope is feasible for the course context;
- out-of-scope elements are explicit and help avoid undue expectations;
- assumptions are realistic and help explain the conditions on which the project depends;
- constraints are specific and useful;
- risks include technical, organizational, and user-related aspects;
- relevant risks include contingency or mitigation actions;
- stakeholders are identified and their roles are clear.

## Connection to later activities

The product vision is used as input for later activities in the course.

In the product backlog activity, students use the product vision to derive user stories and acceptance criteria aligned with the problem, users, and expected value of the system.

In the UML modeling activity, students use the product vision to reason about workflows, components, states, and responsibilities.

In the sustainability-oriented requirements activity, students revisit the product vision to identify sustainability concerns related to the project context. For example, assumptions, constraints, risks, stakeholders, and out-of-scope elements may reveal concerns related to accessibility, workload, data use, maintainability, inclusion, or long-term viability.
