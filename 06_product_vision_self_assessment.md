# Product Vision Self-Assessment Instrument

## Purpose

This document provides an anonymized and adapted version of the self-assessment instrument used to support the review of the product vision artifact.

The purpose of the instrument is to help student teams evaluate whether their product vision is clear, coherent, feasible, and useful as a basis for later Software Engineering activities, including product backlog construction, UML modeling, and sustainability-oriented requirements analysis.

## Anonymization note

The original form included identifying fields such as student name, project name, client name, and contact information. These fields were removed or generalized for double-anonymous review.

This version is provided as a reusable educational artifact.

## Suggested response scale

For each closed question, students may answer:

- Yes
- No
- Partially

Students are encouraged to add comments whenever they answer "No" or "Partially".

The product vision should identify potential problems that may affect the project.

## Connection to later activities

The product vision is used as input for later activities in the course.

In the product backlog activity, students use the product vision to derive user stories and acceptance criteria aligned with the problem, users, scope, and expected value of the system.

In the UML modeling activity, students use the product vision to reason about workflows, components, states, responsibilities, and system boundaries.

In the sustainability-oriented requirements activity, students revisit the product vision to identify sustainability concerns. Assumptions, constraints, risks, stakeholders, scope, and out-of-scope elements may reveal concerns related to accessibility, workload, privacy, maintainability, inclusion, resource use, or long-term viability.

## Common issues to avoid

Students should pay attention to the following issues:

- problem descriptions that are too vague;
- objectives that describe features but not expected benefits;
- scope descriptions that are too detailed or too broad;
- missing out-of-scope elements;
- unrealistic assumptions;
- constraints that are too generic;
- risks without contingency or mitigation actions;
- stakeholders described only by name, without clear roles;
- lack of coherence among justification, objective, scope, and stakeholders;
- product vision written as a technical specification rather than as a high-level project artifact.
